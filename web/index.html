Can fossil energy and forest cohabit ?

People need trees and power plants to survive — How can they co- share the world?

Sustainability is a complex, multivariate problem. Yet in public debate it is too often reduced to a single number: carbon emissions, deforestation, suburbanization. Each metric tells part of the story — none tells all of it.

We explore three forces that can shape the outcome — energy, forests, people. This website puts them together on the same map.

Patterns reveal themselves when you put the data together.

Across 148 countries, a coal-heavy grid does not always sit on deforested land; a hydro-dominated grid does not always protect trees. Energy mix and forest loss overlap in some places, diverge in others — and that difference is what policy must address.

Conclusion · What the data tells

I.

Energy type does not predict deforestation

Global · energy and deforestation correlation

Across every region visible on this map, the dominant fuel mix of a country is a poor predictor of its deforestation pressure. Brazil runs on 68 % hydro and leads the world in forest loss. Indonesia burns 88 % fossil fuels, but the forest dies for palm oil and pulp, not for power plants alone. Siberia loses boreal taiga to climate-driven wildfire, not to coal mines next door.

The link between fossil fuels and deforestation is systemic and global, not local and direct. Carbon emissions warm the climate, warming ignites forests. Industrial supply chains that extract coal also open roads into previously inaccessible interiors.

Each dot below is a country. The horizontal axis shows how much of its electricity comes from fossil fuels; the vertical axis shows what share of its deforestation is definitive — commodity agriculture, shifting cultivation, and urbanization, the causes that leave no forest behind.

Each dot = one country · size = deforestation pixels · color = dominant fuel · hover for details

If fossil energy caused deforestation, the dots would climb from left to right. They do not. High fossil shares appear at every level of definitive deforestation, and vice versa. The forest disappears for land, not for power — clearing driven by commodity agriculture and urban sprawl that operates independently of the energy mix.

"Renewable grids and vanishing forests coexist as often as coal grids and intact ones."
II.

We are operating beyond the planet's safe limits

Planetary Boundaries · Stockholm Resilience Centre · 2023

The Stockholm Resilience Centre identifies nine planetary boundaries — biophysical thresholds that define a "safe operating space" for humanity. Every power plant and every deforestation pixel on this map contributes to two boundaries already breached.

Radiative Forcing At risk

~2.9 W/m² observed · safe boundary 1.0 W/m² — CO₂ from fossil combustion is the primary driver.

Biosphere Integrity (HANPP) Exceeded

>75 % HANPP observed · safe boundary <25 % — land-use change and deforestation have consumed three-quarters of the planet's biological production.

These two boundaries are not independent: deforestation reduces the terrestrial carbon sink, accelerating radiative forcing; climate warming dries and ignites forests, accelerating biosphere integrity loss. The two crises amplify each other — and each dot on this map sits somewhere on that feedback loop.

Sources: Rockström et al., Nature 2009 · Richardson et al., Science Advances 2023 · Stockholm Resilience Centre
