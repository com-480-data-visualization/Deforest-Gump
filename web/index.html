Where energy meets the forest.

Three pressures share one map — power, people and trees — and the pattern only reveals itself when you look at all of them at once.

BY SIMON BARRAS, OLIVER DAOUD & PHUC-HUNG TRUONG · Editorial Desk
Filed May 26, 2026 · 7 min read · Interactive

Sustainability is a complex, multivariate problem. Yet in public debate it is too often reduced to a single number: carbon emissions. That shorthand offers a framework anyone can grasp — and, in the same breath, it obscures everything else that holds the living world together.

Our objective with this dispatch is to narrow the knowledge gap through smart, interactive visualizations. We follow three entangled forces — energy production, deforestation, and urbanization — and put them in conversation with the sustainability metrics they shape.

High deforestation does not always mean high fossil fuels. The map tells a more interesting story.

Across 148 countries, the data reveals patterns that no single indicator can capture alone. A coal-heavy grid does not always sit on cleared land; a hydro-dominated grid does not always protect it. The geography of energy and the geography of forest loss overlap in places, diverge in others — and the difference matters for the policies meant to address them.

What follows is an atlas in three field reports, then an interactive table on which the reader is invited to draw their own lines. Filter by fuel, overlay deforestation, zoom into a basin. The data is the same on every screen; the questions you bring to it are not.

Below, three regions that — together — outline the full shape of the problem.

Energy type does not predict deforestation cause.

The chart shows the share of countries in the current view where each fuel type co-exists with deforestation. Renewable fuels — hydro, wind, solar — appear alongside deforestation nearly as often as coal or gas. Brazil's hydro-dominated grid yet world-leading forest loss is the clearest example: the pressure comes from commodity agriculture, not power plants.
